The strategy

Staging vs. Photography

There's a 90% overlap between staging and photography prep. The other 10% is where photography requires a completely different approach.

The camera sees differently

What feels warm and inviting in person reads as clutter on camera. Decorative items that stagers love are exactly what the lens reveals as noise.

Lights, Bulbs & Screens

The camera reveals every lighting mistake. Get these right first — they affect every shot in the house.

0 of 4 done

Every overhead, every lamp, every under-cabinet light. Mixed natural and artificial light warms interiors on camera — don't skip small lamps.

Let natural light flood in. Only keep blinds closed if there's something distracting or unflattering outside the window.

A mix of cool-blue and warm-yellow bulbs in the same room is visible on camera even when your eye ignores it. Consistency matters.

Black screens photograph far better than a frozen mid-frame. Same applies to computer monitors and any projector displays.

De-clutter Personal Items

Anything on a flat surface that isn't part of the house should disappear. Start with the kitchen — it's the highest-impact room.

0 of 4 done

Appliances, utensils, towels, trash cans, dog bowls, fridge magnets — all of it. Bare counters read as spacious and clean on camera.

Photo frames, faux plants, clocks, and small decor from tables, mantles, dressers, and desks. Buyers want to picture their own life here.

Toiletries, appliances, tissue boxes, soap dispensers — completely clear. The master bathroom carries as much weight as the kitchen.

Small rugs break up the visual continuity of flooring. Rooms read larger and more cohesive without them interrupting the sightlines.

Stop the Motion

Anything that moves causes blur or distraction. Two quick items handle most of it — don't overlook either.

0 of 2 done

As cute as they are — garage, kennel, or offsite. Wagging tails and moving bodies blur the frame and require retakes.

Some fans take 5 minutes to fully stop spinning. Turn them off well before the photographer arrives so blades are completely still.

Curb Appeal

The exterior is usually the first photo a buyer sees. Don't let a car, a yard sign, or a pile of leaves steal the frame from the house.

0 of 5 done

Into the garage, onto a side street, or around the block. Anywhere but the driveway or directly in front of the house.

A fresh cut dramatically changes how the exterior reads on camera. Confirm beds look tidy and hedges are shaped.

Rake the lawn, sweep patios, walkways, and driveways. Nothing ages a photo faster than a pile of leaves or scattered debris.

For Sale, security, school spirit, seasonal — all of them. Signs date a photo and add visual clutter the eye can't ignore.

A power-washed driveway can transform an exterior shot. At minimum, sweep all paved surfaces clean of dirt and stains.

Backyard & Outdoor

The backyard is where buyers imagine their next weekend. Stage it like you're about to entertain — open, clean, aspirational.

0 of 4 done

Vacuum, skim, and pull every float, toy, and cleaning pole off the water. A clean, still pool is a calm and aspirational shot.

Stage it how you'd want it photographed. Open umbrellas read as usable and inviting. Arrange chairs to create a natural gathering feel.

Garden hoses, brooms, chemical bottles, trash and recycle bins — all out of frame. If you'd put it away for a dinner party, put it away for this.

Screens reduce light transmission and can distort window frames in photos. Remove or minimize where possible without compromising the shoot setup.

Quick reminder

Treat it like
the in-laws are coming.

  • Don't just tidy up — thoroughly clean
  • Hide items in places that won't be photographed
  • Less = more. Way less = way more
  • Kitchen and master bath are the top priority
  • Closets, cabinets, drawers — use them
  • Review images with your photographer on-site
