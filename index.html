The strategy

Staging vs. Photography

There's a 90% overlap between staging and photography prep. The other 10% is where photography requires a completely different approach.

The camera sees differently

What feels warm and inviting in person reads as clutter on camera. Decorative items that stagers love are exactly what the lens reveals as noise.

Real Estate Photography

Light & Visibility

0 of 5 done

Light is the first thing buyers feel — before they read a single word.

Dark rooms photograph small.

Natural light is free staging. Let it in.

Mixed color temps read as neglect on camera.

A black screen disappears. A frozen one steals the frame.

Spinning blades blur every shot.

Clear the Space

0 of 5 done

Buyers are purchasing your square footage, not your belongings.

The emptier it looks, the larger it photographs.

Think boutique hotel — counters, tub ledges, shower floors.

Buyers need to picture their life here, not yours.

Bare floors photograph larger and read cleaner.

Garage, kennel, or offsite. Beds, bowls, toys too.

Curb Appeal

0 of 4 done

The exterior is the first photo every buyer sees. Make them stop scrolling.

A parked car halves the shot.

Crisp yards signal turnkey.

Leaves and debris date a photo instantly.

Signs date the listing photos.

Outdoor & Backyard

0 of 4 done

Buyers are buying a lifestyle. Show them the one they want.

Still water reads as luxury. Every float and pole out.

Stage it like you're hosting.

If you'd hide it for a dinner party, hide it now.

Screens reduce light and distort clean lines.

Quick reminder

Treat it like
the in-laws are coming.

  • Don't just tidy up — thoroughly clean
  • Hide items in places that won't be photographed
  • Less = more. Way less = way more
  • Kitchen and master bath are the top priority
  • Closets, cabinets, drawers — use them
  • Review images with your photographer on-site
